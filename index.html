01   About

An 18-year-old asking questions most people wait decades to ask, about the nature of space, expansion, and cosmic order.

I am an independent researcher from India, currently developing and testing Configurational Dispersal Cosmology (CDC) — a framework that proposes cosmic acceleration may arise from an intrinsic dispersal tendency in the universe's configuration space, rather than from an external dark energy component.

After school, I took a gap year to work independently and used that time for self-directed research in cosmology, mathematics, and numerical methods. Coming from a low-income background, I have treated the work carefully: building the framework step by step, documenting what is established, and separating formal development from claims that require observation.

In January 2026, Emergent Ventures funding allowed me to continue the research full-time and gain access to computing resources. The current phase focuses on CDC II's mathematical framework, numerical evolution of the derived background system, and preparation for CAMB / CLASS integration and later likelihood-based testing.

05   Approach

I am drawn to ideas that are structurally different from the consensus, not for the sake of it, but because the most important shifts in understanding tend to look that way at the start.

Paradigm-shifting ideas often appear abnormal before they appear correct. This is not a justification for bad science; it is a reason to hold unconventional ideas to a higher standard of rigour, not a lower one. If the idea is genuinely different, the burden of demonstration is larger, not smaller.

I try to be honest about what is established and what is not. The difference between "this framework has an interesting structure" and "this framework is correct" is significant, and I take that difference seriously. Preliminary results are not results. Intuitions are not evidence.

The goal of working on CDC is not to be right about dark energy. The goal is to build something that is genuinely testable: one that makes predictions which could, in principle, falsify it. A framework that cannot be falsified is not doing science, regardless of how elegant it is.

I am 18 and work without institutional affiliation or formal supervision. What I have is time, access to the same literature as everyone else, and a willingness to do the slow work: learning the tools, checking the mathematics, and testing against real data.
